Z's Barbershop · Phase 5 — Logo Concepts

Four directions for the heritage seal. All in brass. All with cedar. Different on shape, formality, and how cedar integrates.

Direction A's brand identity needs a heritage-seal-with-cedar logo that works at three scales: large standalone (250px+), nav-header (40–60px), and favicon (32×32). Each concept below shows all three, on dark backgrounds (the dominant context) and cream backgrounds (for cards + the case-study deliverable).

These are first-pass SVG drafts. Pick a direction, then we refine the winner — adjust proportions, fine-tune the cedar silhouette, tweak the lockup. Any of the four can become final.

01

The Arched Seal

Most traditional

Classic heritage barbershop seal in an arched frame. Cedar tree at the apex, "Z'S" monogram in oversized Big Shoulders, "BARBERSHOP" arched along the bottom, "EST. WESTCHESTER · MIAMI" beneath. Closest to House of Heritage's seal vocabulary. Reads "this place has been here for a while" instantly.

02

The Damascene Roundel

Most distinctly Lebanese

Circular seal with an 8-pointed Damascene star pattern as the outer border ring. "Z'S" monogram dead center. Cedar integrated into the ring — replacing one of the 8 star points, or as a tiny detail in the center field. Folds heritage anchor #5 (Damascene pattern) directly into the logo itself. Most distinctly Lebanese-Mediterranean of the four.

Strengths

  • Most distinctive — circular roundels are uncommon in barbershop branding
  • Folds heritage anchor #5 (Damascene pattern) directly into the logo, freeing the rest of the site from carrying the pattern repeatedly
  • Roundels favicon-down better than arched seals (circle survives at 32px)
  • Mediterranean / Lebanese coding is the strongest of the four — Damascene star is universally recognized

Tradeoffs

  • The Damascene star is so distinctive it doesn't fold easily back into "barbershop" — could read like a coffee shop or rug merchant if photography isn't dialed
  • Cedar is harder to spot (smaller inside the inner circle)
  • Refinement needs care — the two overlapping squares can look busy if not perfectly aligned
03

Type Lockup with Cedar

Most minimal · scales best

No frame. Pure typography: massive "Z'S BARBERSHOP" in Big Shoulders Display 800, with the cedar tree positioned as a fixed-width element to the left of "Z'S" — almost like a serif drop-cap. The cedar IS the only decorative element. Works smallest. Modern feel inside the heritage register.

Strengths

  • Scales down the best of the four — cedar alone works as the favicon
  • Most modern feel inside the heritage register — doesn't lean costume-y

  • No frame = no clear "this is the logo" container, sometimes ambiguous in busy layouts
  • Doesn't read "barbershop" without the wordmark; harder to use without text
04

Vintage Sign Plate

Most "old neighborhood"

Rectangular Americana barbershop sign aesthetic. "Z'S BARBERSHOP" stamped across center. Cedar in the left corner as a corner ornament; "EST. WESTCHESTER" stamped right corner. Reads "this sign has been hanging outside the shop for thirty years." Most "old neighborhood institution" of the four — matches Z's actual 5-star reputation built over a decade.

What happens next

Pick a direction (1 / 2 / 3 / 4). The winner gets refined — adjust proportions, fine-tune the cedar silhouette, tighten the lockup, finalize the SVG paths. Refinement is interactive: you'll see V2, V3, etc. until it lands.

Specifically what to think about when picking:

  • How important is favicon readability? Concept 3 wins (cedar alone scales). Concept 2 next (roundel survives). Concepts 1 and 4 lose detail at 32px.
  • How "Lebanese" should the visual identity feel? Concept 2 is most distinctly Lebanese/Mediterranean. Concept 4 is most generic-Americana. Concepts 1 and 3 are middle ground.
  • How important is a sealed "logo lockup"? Concepts 1, 2, 4 have framed lockups. Concept 3 is unframed type-and-cedar.
  • Where will the logo physically appear? Awning sign? Business card? Instagram? Shop window decal? Concept 4 already looks like a sign. Concept 2 lives best in digital. Concept 3 is most flexible.

If none feel right, tell me what's off — I'll iterate. Concept 5, mash-ups, completely different direction — all on the table.

The cedar silhouette across all four is a hand-coded SVG path that's intentionally a rough draft. Final version gets a cleaner, more recognizably-Lebanese cedar shape in the refinement pass.
